About Dani Elle

I was born in Jamaica and raised on stories. Not just the ones in books -- the ones my family told at the table, the ones passed between women in kitchens and on porches. Storytelling, for me, has always been how people hold on to each other.

By nine years old I was already writing. Not because someone told me to -- because I couldn't not. I had things to say about love, about women, about what it costs to be soft in a world that rewards armor.

My early work leaned hard into romance -- Black love, heat, instant connection, the kind of stories that make you fan yourself. I'm not apologizing for that. That work is real and it matters and it's still me. But I've been growing. My fiction is finding a deeper register -- women standing in the wreckage of their own perfect lives and choosing themselves anyway.

Breaking the Good Wife is that evolution in motion. It's still got Dani Elle's fingerprints all over it -- the wit, the heat, the honesty -- but it's asking bigger questions. About sacrifice. About identity. About what you become when you stop shrinking.

I live between my Jamaican roots and my American routes, and that's exactly where my stories live too.

"I write the women who are done being small."

Contemporary women's fiction and romance rooted in Black love, Jamaican soul, and the kind of truth that stings a little.

Breaking the Good Wife
Sometimes "not enough" is the beginning.
Women's Fiction Black Love Reinvention Betrayal

Some women fight for their marriage. Kennesha Forbes is done fighting. For eight years, she molded herself into the perfect wife -- cutting off friends, turning down opportunities, making herself smaller so Fitzroy Scott could shine. She thought their wedding would be a fresh start. Instead, at a bus stop on a Monday morning, he asks for a divorce. No explanation. No real reason. Just: "You're not enough."

But when Kennesha discovers the truth behind his betrayal, "not enough" becomes her battle cry.
